Marine Park Map
Source: Marine Park Alliance

Marine Park is located in the south-eastern corner of Brooklyn, and is the largest public park in Brooklyn, with an area of 3,229,391 sq m (Source: NYC Parks). There are three main sections to the entire park: Marine Park proper, Gerritsen Beach proper, and the golf course and adjoining salt marsh and uplands. The two main buildings within the park are the Salt Marsh Nature Center and the Carmine Carro Community Center. The outdoor facilities provide courts and fields for an array of sports.

1917 - philanthropists Frederick B. Pratt and Alfred T. White gifted the city 607,028 sq m in the area for use as a park.
1920s - The park was built and was originally a tidal marshland.
1930s - It was transformed into a park as part of a large-scale land reclamation project.
1937 - New land purchases increased the park’s area to 7,373,372 sq m. The Board of Aldermen named the site “Brooklyn Marine Park.”
1939 - The Pratt-White athletic field was dedicated in tribute to the two fathers of Marine Park.
1963 – A 849,840 sq m golf course opened
1974 – 4,143,980 sq m were transferred to the National Park Service for inclusion in the Gateway National Recreation Area.
1984 - Nature trails established along Gerritsen Creek.
Late 20th century - Ongoing improvements include the reconstruction of basketball, tennis, and bocce courts. 2000 - The Salt Marsh Nature Center opened
2013 - The Carmine Carro Community Center opened

Prospect Park Map
Source: prospectpark.org

Prospect Park is located in the middle of Brooklyn, and is the second largest public park in Brooklyn, behind Marine Park, with an area of 2,128,646 sq m (Source: NYC Parks). The Park is part of the Brooklyn-Queens Greenway, a network of green spaces that stretch across western Long Island. Main attractions include the Long Meadow, the Picnic House, Litchfield Villa, Prospect Park Zoo, the Boathouse, Concert Grove, Brooklyn's only lake, the Prospect Park Bandshell, as well as the sports facilities.

1858 - Prospect Park was laid out by Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux
1867 - The park opened
1873 - The park substantially completed
1935 - A NYC Parks commissioner Robert Moses launch many development projects, including the Prospect Park Zoo, new playgrounds around the Park’s perimeter, etc.
Late 20th century - The Prospect Park Alliance created. Late 1980s, they refurbished many parts of the park.
1975 - The park was designated a New York City scenic landmark.
1980 - The park was listed on the National Register of Historic Places.
1987 - Prospect Park Alliance created.
Mid 1990s - Alliance embarked on a $9 million woodlands restoration
Late 2000s - The southeast corner was reshaped.
2009 - The Alliance announced a radical redesign.
2017- The Alliance began the restoration of the landscapes in northeast corner -- the Vale.

Belt and Shore Parkway Map
Source: nyc.gov

Belt and Shore Parkway is located in the south-west of Brooklyn. The Shore Parkway, Southern Parkway, Laurelton Parkway, and Cross Island Parkway are collectively known as the "Belt System". The Belt Parkway was one of many parkways in the New York metropolitan area built by Robert Moses. Moses considered Shore Parkway, and the rest of the Belt Parkway, to be a “continuous park system, not merely an automobile artery, a system of shoestring parks and recreational facilities encircling the entire metropolis….”.

1930 - The Belt Parkway was proposed by Moses.
1940 - The Belt Parkway System opened.
1941 - A 19,000 m bike path along the Southern Brooklyn section of the Belt Parkway opened

Manhattan Beach Park is located in the South of Brooklyn, and is a popular summertime destination for people from all over New York City.
In 1866, Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux conceived of a network of wide, tree-lined avenues that would cut across the grid of Brooklyn and link its open spaces. Modeled after the Avenue de L’Impératrice (now the Avenue Foch) in Paris, Olmsted and Vaux’s new roads differed from their French predecessors in that they stretched for several miles. They were intended to provide sumptuous green corridors along which citizens could live and take pleasure drives. Two of them were built along Olmsted and Vaux’s newly designed Prospect Park: Eastern Parkway, running from Prospect Park to Crown Heights, and Ocean Parkway, connecting Prospect Park to Coney Island.

1877 - Manhattan Beach was created by real estate developer Austin Corbin as a self-contained summer resort on 500 acres of salt marsh.
1907 - Residential development began
1942 - The land on this site was originally acquired by the Federal Government.
1955 - An additional 24.4 acres were acquired
1997 - It was renovated under a $778,000 capital project funded by Borough President Golden and Councilmember Anthony D. Weiner.

Sunset Park is located in the Southeast of Brooklyn, between 41st and 44th Streets and Fifth and Seventh Avenues. It is 103,195 sq m. From the park’s highest point, the Statue of Liberty, vibrant Brooklyn neighborhoods, and the green hills of Staten Island and New Jersey can be seen. There is an equally diverse terrain: a state-of-the-art swimming pool, green fields, pedestrian walkways, a sand volleyball court, a recreation center, and a living memorial.

Bridge Park, Site Plan
Source: Michael Van Valkenburgh Associates

Brooklyn Bridge Park is a 343,983 park on the Brooklyn side of the East River in New York City. Designed by landscape architecture firm Michael Van Valkenburgh Associates. From north to south, the park includes the preexisting Empire–Fulton Ferry and Main Street Parks; the historic Fulton Ferry Landing; and Piers 1–6, which contain various playgrounds and residential developments.

2002 - After the city and state signed a joint agreement, site planning and project funding proceeded.
2007 - The first work involved the demolition of a warehouse under the Brooklyn Bridge.
2010 - The park's first portion, Pier 1, opened.
2010 - Pier 6, located at the intersection of Furman Street and Atlantic Avenue was the second section of parkland to open.
2012 - Pier 5 officially opened with three athletic fields, two playgrounds and a picnic area.
2013 - the Greenway Terrace opened on the shoreline adjacent to Pier 3.
2014 - A former warehouse dock, the 5-acre (2.0 ha) Pier 2 opened and is dedicated to active recreation.
2014 - Pier 4 was repurposed from a former railroad car float bridge that had rotted and sunk into the East River.
2018 - The park was 90% complete.
